## Design Context

### Users
HR/People Ops VPs and engineering managers reviewing team wellbeing data. They open Sentinel during strategic reviews, before 1:1s, and when investigating concerning patterns. Their emotional state is proactive concern: "Is anyone burning out? Who needs support?" They need quick signals most of the time, with the ability to drill deep when something looks wrong. Trust is paramount: if the tool feels like surveillance, adoption dies.

### Brand Personality
**Calm, precise, trustworthy.** Like a well-calibrated medical instrument that respects both the operator and the patient. Sentinel handles sensitive human data, so every pixel must communicate "we take this seriously" without feeling clinical or cold. The interface should feel like a thoughtful colleague who notices patterns others miss, not a monitoring system that tracks keystrokes.

### Aesthetic Direction
- **Visual tone:** Industrial/utilitarian with warmth. Information-dense but not overwhelming. Serious but not sterile.
- **References:** Lattice (HR warmth, human-centered language) meets Linear (data clarity, precise typography, dark-mode-first craft). Not Datadog (too operational). Not Notion (too playful for sensitive data).
- **Anti-references:** Never look like surveillance software (Teramind, Hubstaff). Never look like generic AI dashboards (purple gradients, glowing cards, "AI-powered" badges). Never look like legacy corporate HR (Workday, SAP).
- **Theme:** Dark-mode default. Dual-theme support exists but dark is primary.
- **Color:** Emerald green as the single accent. Color is rare and meaningful: only for risk badges, active states, and primary actions. Grayscale dominates.
- **Typography:** Geist Sans (display + body), Geist Mono (data/code). Industrial character, high legibility.

### Design Principles

1. **Signal, not surveillance.** Every UI decision must reinforce that Sentinel cares about employees, not monitors them. Use warm, supportive language. Show "wellbeing indicators" not "tracking metrics." When in doubt, ask: "Would an employee feel safe knowing their manager sees this?"

2. **Precision over decoration.** Data should speak for itself. No gradients, no glassmorphism, no decorative elements that don't serve understanding. The math IS the design: show the formula, show the confidence, show the trend. Trust comes from transparency, not polish.

3. **Calm urgency.** Risk levels need to feel important without feeling alarming. CRITICAL should prompt action, not panic. The UI should feel like a smoke detector (clear signal, calm response) not a fire alarm (screaming emergency). Red is for danger, amber for attention, emerald for healthy. No other hues compete.

4. **Metadata-only transparency.** The privacy architecture is a feature, not a footnote. Every data view should make it effortless for users to understand what Sentinel sees and what it doesn't. The two-vault architecture should feel inherent, not bolted on.

5. **Human-scale information.** Dense data is OK, but it must have clear hierarchy. Use the type scale aggressively: big numbers for KPIs, medium text for context, small text for details. White space separates meaning, not just pixels. Every screen should answer "what should I look at first?" within 2 seconds.
